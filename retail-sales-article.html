Retail Sales Show Signs of Stabilizing After Tariff Shock

By Maria Benitez
June 16, 2025 · 7 min read

After a turbulent spring defined by rising consumer prices and a cascade of new tariffs on imports from China and Southeast Asia, May's U.S. retail sales report is offering the first glimmer of stability for retailers — and perhaps for the broader American economy. The Commerce Department reported Monday that retail sales increased by 0.3 percent in May, adjusted for seasonal variation. That figure marks a modest recovery from April's revised 0.1 percent decline and stands out as a signal of resilience in a market still wrestling with inflation, policy uncertainty, and geopolitical shocks.

Department stores led the rebound, with sales rising 1.2 percent — the strongest gain for the sector since last December. Grocery, apparel, and auto parts retailers also posted mild upticks, while sales at gas stations and furniture stores continued to decline. "We're seeing resilience, but the margin is thin," said Rebecca Thornton, a senior economist at Barclays Capital. "Consumers are still spending, but there's a psychological ceiling forming — the sense that anything could tip this balance."

The report arrives after a rocky first quarter in which Washington introduced sweeping tariff hikes — including a 30 percent duty on electric vehicles, semiconductors, and steel imports from China — followed by Beijing's retaliatory measures on American agricultural exports and luxury goods. The policy crossfire created widespread disruptions across supply chains and injected fresh volatility into pricing models for retailers. "March and April were nightmare months for importers," said Sophie Caldwell, Vice President of Trade Compliance at the American Retail Federation. "You had container prices jumping 18 percent in six weeks, and buyers didn't know what the end price would be at point of sale."

Despite the volatility, consumer spending held up better than many economists anticipated — albeit with some shifting behavior. Shoppers leaned into discount outlets, Buy Now, Pay Later (BNPL) platforms, and loyalty-based promotions. Online sales grew 0.6 percent month-over-month, continuing a yearlong trend toward digital-first commerce. Yet brick-and-mortar department stores — long seen as a fading retail format — enjoyed a rare surge, driven by aggressive clearance sales and holiday weekend foot traffic. "There's pent-up demand," said Michael Jeong, chief financial officer of a mid-sized department store chain based in St. Louis. "Customers were waiting for prices to stabilize. The moment they felt confident inflation was cooling, they came back."

Inflation has begun to ease but remains a prominent concern for households. The consumer price index slowed to 3.1 percent year-over-year in May, the lowest reading since September 2023. Food prices remained flat for the third consecutive month, and energy prices dipped slightly. However, core inflation, which excludes food and energy, stayed firm at 3.8 percent, largely due to persistent increases in rent and service costs. "The price of the grocery basket may be flattening, but rent and healthcare are still climbing," said Marla Chan, a senior policy analyst at the Urban Economics Institute. "That squeezes out discretionary spending, especially for lower-income households."

Data from JPMorgan Chase showed spending by the bottom income quartile declined for a third consecutive month, even as upper-quartile spending ticked upward. This divergence is troubling, analysts say, as it reveals a widening gap in consumer confidence and flexibility. Federal Reserve officials, meanwhile, opted to keep interest rates unchanged at 5.25 percent during their June meeting, citing the need for more data before adjusting monetary policy. "This is exactly the kind of soft landing scenario Chair Powell wants," said Dr. Anika Roy, a macroeconomics professor at the University of Chicago. "Slow, steady growth. Slowing inflation. But any external shock — another trade war escalation, energy price spike, credit crunch — could derail that."

Retailers remain cautious as they enter the summer season, with the critical back-to-school and early holiday buying periods on the horizon. Inventories remain high in some categories, particularly electronics and furniture — sectors most exposed to the spring's tariff volatility. Best Buy and Lowe's have already revised their Q3 earnings forecasts downward, while Walmart and Target have upgraded their guidance on the strength of grocery and household essentials. "Consumers are telling us they're not done spending — they're just being much smarter about it," said Janelle Reed, Chief Insights Officer at RetailNext, a retail intelligence firm.

The broader question now is whether May's modest rebound signals the beginning of a durable recovery or merely a temporary pause before a deeper slowdown. "There's no clean narrative right now," Thornton added. "We have a soft landing in progress — but no guarantees." For now, the American consumer — long the workhorse of the U.S. economy — continues forward cautiously, balancing necessity with restraint, and adapting to each shift in prices and policy with quiet persistence. And for retailers hoping for stability in the second half of 2025, that may be the most realistic outcome to hope for.
